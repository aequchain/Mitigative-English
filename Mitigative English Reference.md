Minimalist Direct Language (Added 2025-10-06)

Through iterative proofing of foundation examples, following linguistic pattern emerged as optimal for training data:
Article Minimization

Remove articles (a, an, the) wherever possible without compromising clarity. This creates telegraphic precision and maximizes information density per token.

Examples:

    "A garden designer" → "Garden designer"
    "the pathway" → "pathway"
    "an elegant connection" → "connection"
    "the entire forest" → "entire forest"

Word Choice Precision

Prefer technical, specific terms over casual or general ones:

    "partnership/relationship" → "collaboration"
    "connection" → "path"
    "the whole" → "complete"

Verb Form Directness

Use present continuous and active forms for ongoing processes:

    "contributes" → "contributing"
    "form a relationship" → "have collaboration"

Structural Simplification

    Remove unnecessary descriptors unless technically essential
    Prefer singular to plural where meaning allows ("tree" vs "multiple trees")
    Use direct phrasing over prepositional complexity ("need of others" vs "others in need")
    Lead with observable qualities when relevant ("Observable mutualistic collaboration")

Rationale

This minimalist approach:

    Maximizes information density in training data
    Removes linguistic padding and filler
    Creates clearer patterns for model learning
    Aligns with Quality & Polish principle (every word earns its place)
    Enhances professional, technical communication style
    Reduces token count while maintaining full meaning

Extended Temporal Language Precision (Added 2025-10-08)

Building on Principle #9 and Mitigative Language patterns, comprehensive temporal guidelines emerged through application:
Ongoing Positive States - Present Continuous

When describing positive, ongoing processes or states:

Prefer: Present continuous tense ("-ing" forms)

Examples:

    "systems grow" → "systems are growing"
    "knowledge expands" → "knowledge is expanding"
    "capabilities develop" → "capabilities are developing"

Rationale: Present continuous emphasizes ongoing nature and active progression of positive states, creating sense of momentum and vitality.
Completed Positive Achievements - Present Perfect

When positive developments have completed and results continue into present:

Prefer: Present perfect ("have/has" + past participle)

Examples:

    "we solved this" → "we have solved this"
    "teams created solutions" → "teams have created solutions"
    "research revealed patterns" → "research has revealed patterns"

Rationale: Present perfect connects past achievement to present value, emphasizing ongoing relevance and continued benefit.
Future Positive Projections - Present Continuous or Simple Future

When describing anticipated positive developments:

Prefer: Present continuous (for near-certain futures) or simple future (for projections)

Examples:

    "will improve" → "is improving" (if already in motion)
    "could enhance" → "will enhance" (when confidence is high)
    "might develop" → "is developing" (when process has begun)

Rationale: Present continuous for near-term positives creates immediacy; simple future maintains clarity for projections. Both frame positivity as approaching reality.
Historical Negative Events - Simple Past

When referencing negative historical events necessarily:

Prefer: Simple past tense, single mention, minimal elaboration

Examples:

    "System faced difficulties" (simple past, singular event)
    "Components experienced stress" (simple past, completed)

Rationale: Simple past places negative events firmly in history, completed and addressed. Minimal elaboration prevents dwelling.
Resolved Challenges - Past Perfect → Present State

When describing challenges that have been addressed:

Prefer: Past perfect for challenge, present tense for current state

Examples:

    "After system had faced difficulties, it now operates smoothly"
    "Components that had experienced stress are now performing optimally"

Rationale: Past perfect creates distance from challenge; present tense emphasizes current positive reality.


### Conditional Negatives - Subjunctive Mood

When describing hypothetical negative scenarios:

**Prefer:** Subjunctive mood (if/were/would) with minimal detail

**Examples:**
- "if failure occurs" → "if variation were to occur" (subjunctive, hypothetical)
- "when errors happen" → "were errors to occur" (subjunctive, less certain)
- "problems will arise" → "if challenges were to emerge" (subjunctive, conditional)

**Rationale:** Subjunctive mood creates psychological distance from negative possibilities, framing them as hypothetical rather than expected realities.


### Sequential Process Description - Progressive Tenses

When describing sequences of positive developments:

**Prefer:** Progressive tenses throughout sequence to emphasize continuity

**Examples:**
- "First we analyzed, then we improved" → "First we were analyzing, then we were improving"
- "Team completed research, created solutions" → "Team was completing research, was creating solutions"

**Rationale:** Progressive forms throughout create sense of flowing, continuous advancement rather than discrete separate steps.


### Timeless Truths - Simple Present

For universal principles and timeless patterns:

**Prefer:** Simple present tense

**Examples:**
- "Knowledge enables growth" (timeless principle)
- "Collaboration creates excellence" (universal pattern)
- "Transparency builds trust" (enduring truth)

**Rationale:** Simple present establishes principles as timeless, fundamental truths rather than temporal observations.


### Application Summary:

- **Ongoing positives** → Present continuous ("-ing")
- **Completed positives** → Present perfect ("have/has")
- **Future positives** → Present continuous (near) or simple future (far)
- **Past negatives** → Simple past, minimal detail
- **Resolved challenges** → Past perfect (challenge) + present (resolution)
- **Hypothetical negatives** → Subjunctive mood ("if/were/would")
- **Process sequences** → Progressive throughout
- **Universal truths** → Simple present

## Causation and Mechanism Language (Added 2025-10-08)

Building on established "vector" terminology for mathematical/technical connections, comprehensive framework for describing causation, mechanisms, and relationships emerged.


### Causation Vectors and Pathways

**For clear directional causation:**

**Prefer:** "vector", "pathway", "mechanism"

**Examples:**
- "X causes Y" → "X creates vector to Y"
- "A leads to B" → "pathway from A to B"
- "results in" → "establishes vector toward"

**Rationale:** "Vector" implies direction and can be quantified. "Cause" is vaguer and often implies singular pathway.


### Multi-Pathway Causation

**When multiple factors contribute:**

**Prefer:** "convergent vectors", "multiple pathways", "contributing factors"

**Examples:**
- "caused by A, B, and C" → "emerges from convergent vectors: A, B, and C"
- "many factors lead to X" → "multiple pathways establish conditions for X"
- "results from combination" → "emerges from pathway integration"

**Rationale:** Complex outcomes rarely have single causes. Multi-pathway language reflects reality more accurately.


### Bidirectional and Reciprocal Pathways

**For mutual causation:**

**Prefer:** "reciprocal vectors", "bidirectional pathway", "mutual enhancement"

**Examples:**
- "A and B influence each other" → "A and B have reciprocal vectors"
- "feedback loop" → "reciprocal enhancement pathway"
- "mutually reinforcing" → "bidirectional amplification vectors"

**Rationale:** Many systems involve mutual causation. Bidirectional language captures this complexity.

### Enabling Conditions vs Direct Mechanisms

**Distinguish between necessary conditions and direct mechanisms:**

**Enabling conditions:** "creates conditions for", "enables", "makes possible"
**Direct mechanisms:** "produces", "generates", "creates"

**Examples:**
- "Temperature enables reaction" (condition) vs "Catalyst produces product" (direct)
- "Training creates conditions for expertise" (enabling) vs "Practice generates skill" (direct)

**Rationale:** Not all causal factors operate identically. Distinguishing types clarifies mechanism understanding.


### Emergent Causation

**For emergent properties:**

**Prefer:** "emerges from", "arises through", "forms from combination of"

**Examples:**
- "complexity causes unpredictability" → "unpredictability emerges from complexity"
- "interaction produces emergence" → "emergent properties arise through interaction"

**Rationale:** Emergent properties aren't "caused" in simple sense—they arise from system dynamics. Language should reflect emergence.


### Sequential Mechanism Chains

**For multi-step causal chains:**

**Prefer:** "sequential pathway", "mechanism chain", "step-wise vector"

**Examples:**
- "A causes B which causes C" → "sequential pathway: A → B → C"
- "chain reaction" → "cascading mechanism chain"
- "domino effect" → "sequential propagation pathway"

**Rationale:** Chains involve multiple steps each with own mechanism. Sequential language clarifies structure.


### Probabilistic Causation

**When causation is probabilistic rather than deterministic:**

**Prefer:** "increases likelihood of", "raises probability of", "enhances chance of"

**Examples:**
- "smoking causes cancer" → "smoking greatly increases cancer likelihood"
- "X results in Y" → "X raises probability of Y"

**Rationale:** Most biological and social causation is probabilistic. Language should reflect uncertainty.

- "single X" → "one single X"
- "small device" → "one small device"
- "researcher team" → "one researcher team"

**Rationale:** "Single" describes uniqueness quality; "one" provides numerical fact. Both serve distinct clarity functions.


#### 2. Technical Terminology Introduction

When using specialized terms unfamiliar to general audience:

**Apply:** Introductory phrase before technical term

**Options:**
- "something called [term]"
- "known as [term]"
- "termed [term]"
- "labeled [term]"

**Examples:**
- "produced frequency comb" → "produced something called frequency comb"
- "using CRISPR technology" → "using technology known as CRISPR"

**Rationale:** Signals to reader: "specialized terminology follows—don't feel uncertain if unfamiliar."


#### 3. Achievement Completion Tense

When describing completed innovations, discoveries, or accomplishments:

**Apply:** Past tense forms over continuous/participial forms

**Pattern:**
- "creating system that transforms" → "created system that transformed"
- "researcher developing treatment" → "researcher developed treatment"
- "team building device transforming" → "team built device that transformed"

**Rationale:** Completed achievements deserve completed verb forms. Emphasizes accomplishment over ongoing process.


#### 4. Result-First Possessive Structure

When describing creator-creation relationships:

**Apply:** Place creation as subject with possessive phrase, rather than creator as subject with action verb

**Pattern:**
- "Researchers created chip" → "One chip of researchers creations"
- "Team developed algorithm" → "One algorithm of team developments"
- "Scientists discovered pathway" → "One pathway of scientists discoveries"

**Rationale:** Emphasizes achievement (chip) over action (creating). Achievement becomes sentence focal point.


#### 5. Zero-Inference Standard

Before finalizing any sentence:

**Evaluation Questions:**
- Could reader miscount quantities? → Add explicit numbers
- Could reader be unfamiliar with terminology? → Add introductory phrase
- Could reader be unclear if action completed? → Use past tense
- Could reader miss achievement emphasis? → Result-first structure
- Could reader need to infer anything? → State explicitly

**Rationale:** If cognitive work required from reader, writer hasn't completed task. Writing serves reader—remove all inferential burden.


### Application Example:

**Standard GP2 Writing:**
"Scientists developed neural interface enabling direct brain-computer communication using novel electrode array"

**Explicit Clarity Maximization Applied:**
"One neural interface of scientists developments enabled direct brain-computer communication using something called novel electrode array"

**Transformation Steps:**
1. Quantification: "interface" → "One neural interface"
2. Possessive restructure: "Scientists developed" → "of scientists developments"
3. Achievement tense: "enabling" → "enabled"
4. Technical term intro: "novel electrode array" → "something called novel electrode array"


### Integration with Existing GP2:

This principle operates as overlay on existing minimalism guidelines:

- **Minimalism** removes unnecessary words (articles, excessive description)
- **Explicit Clarity** adds necessary precision (quantifiers, term introductions)

**Balance Point:** Remove inferential requirements while maintaining telegraphic efficiency.

When principles seem conflicting:
- Minimalism says: "remove 'the'"
- Explicitness says: "add 'one'"
- **Resolution:** Both serve clarity—remove articles (low information), add quantifiers (high information)


### Summary:

**Zero-Inference Communication Standard ensures:**
- Explicit quantification for all countable singulars
- Technical terminology introduction for unfamiliar terms
- Completed verb tenses for finished achievements
- Result-first possessive structures emphasizing outputs
- Complete elimination of reader inferential burden

**Rationale:** Clarity through completeness. Reader serves understanding—writer removes all cognitive barriers to comprehension.

## Letter-Sequence and Word-Ending Pattern DONT USE/avoidances (Added 2025-10-10)

Beyond whole-word DONT USE/avoidance, certain letter sequences and word endings create unintended patterns when parsed. These require single documentation for awareness, complete DONT USE/avoidance going forward.


### Word-Ending Patterns to DONT USE

**-lytic patterns:**
- DONT USE words containing "-lytic" 
- Prefer alternatives: "joining mechanisms", "mechanisms of gathering", "examination methods", "separation methods"

**-ring (present tense):**
- DONT USE present participles ending in "-ring" where possible
- Prefer alternatives: "offers", "refers", "confers", "transfers" or restructure entirely

**-ly and -ily adverb endings:**
- DONT USE adverbs formed with these endings
- "with speed", "at rapid pace", "in quick manner" instead of quick+ly
- "with ease", "without difficulty" instead of easi+ly
- "in direct manner", "without intermediary" instead of direct+ly
- "in specific manner", "with precision" instead of specifi+cally


### Letter-Proximity Patterns

**Swear word letter proximities:**
- DONT USE F, U, C in close sequence or proximity
- Check words and phrases for unintended letter patterns
- Restructure sentences to prevent proximity patterns


### Word-Specific Preferences

**"focus" usage:**
- Prefer "concentration" over this word
- DONT USE "attention" 
- Alternative phrasings: "concentrate on", "center on", "direct toward", "examine"

**Time-of-day words:**
- DONT USE "morning"

- Acceptable: "evening", "dusk"


### Additional Word-Specific DONT USE/avoidances (Added 2025-10-10)

**Demand/need/require:**
- DONT USE these verbs implying compulsion
- Prefer: "necessary", "necessitate", "necessitated"
- Examples: "requires attention" → "necessitates attention", "system needs" → "system necessitates"

**Fuel:**
- DONT USE this word entirely
- Seek alternatives: "energy source", "power source", "propellant", "energy supply"

**Photon:**
- DONT USE "-ton" ending
- Prefer: "light quantum" (singular), "light quanta" (plural)

**Particle and similar -ticle words:**
- DONT USE "-ticle" ending
- Alternatives: "quantum" (physics), "unit" (general), "element" (chemistry)
- Context-specific: "electron", "proton", "neutron" (already DONT USE -ton where possible)

**Phosphate:**
- DONT USE "-hate" ending and word "fate" (contains "-ate" ending)
- Alternative: "phosphorus compound", "phosphoryl group", "PO₄ group"
- When technical necessity exists: use once with immediate transition to alternatives

**Analysis/analyze:**
- DONT USE these forms
- Prefer: "evaluate", "evaluation", "examine", "examination", "study"
- "analytical" → "evaluative", "examination-based"

**Relationship/relationships:**
- Already documented in Precision Word Choice Standards (v5)
- Reiteration: Use context-specific alternatives
- Mathematical/technical: "vector", "function", "correspondence", "linkage"
- Cooperative: "collaboration", "partnership"
- Causal: "pathway", "mechanism"

3. Check for demand/need/require, fuel, analysis forms, relationship
4. Replace problematic words with alternatives
5. Restructure rather than preserve problematic patterns
6. When technical necessity exists: single use with immediate alternatives thereafter

**Rationale:** Letter-level parsing can reveal unintended patterns even in technically correct words. Expanding DONT USE/avoidance vocabulary ensures clean training data.
